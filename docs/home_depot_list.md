## SECTION 1 — RECOMMENDED DESIGN SUMMARY

**Foam-and-deck catamaran, Size B** (500mm pontoons, 350mm beam):

| Component | Material | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Pontoons | 6× pool noodles (3/side), 500mm, on a ½" PVC core rod each | Foam needs zero waterproofing, is fully reusable, and gives tunable buoyancy (add/remove noodles). The PVC core rod is the structural spine — foam alone would sag under deck load. |
| Deck | ¾" plywood/project-panel, ~350×200mm cut from a larger panel | Flat surface bolts the 220×170mm enclosure directly with no adapter; leftover panel is genuinely reusable shop stock (the strongest "reuse after demo" material in the design). |
| Mast | ¾" pine dowel, ~500mm | Lightest option keeps center of gravity low — matters for tip stability in a small catamaran. |
| Fasteners | UV zip ties (noodle↔rod, and deck↔noodle-bundle through drilled deck holes), stainless M4 (enclosure↔deck), one drilled aluminum angle bracket (mast↔deck) | No PVC cement, no structural epoxy cure gate — everything is mechanical, so there's no 24hr cure wait blocking Week 6's schedule. |

**Buoyancy:** 6 noodles at 500mm give ≈4.7kg lift at half-submersion vs. a 2.5kg loaded-weight target → **1.9× margin at rest, ~3.7× fully submerged.** (Full math in the Part 1 design analysis.)

**Estimated total hull cost (Home Depot, verified pricing): ~$86–92 before tax** — see Section 6. This is **over the original $36 back-of-envelope estimate** because Home Depot sells in fixed pack/sheet sizes (12-pack noodles, 10-packs of hardware, full project panels) rather than the per-unit dollar-store pricing the design math assumed. See Section 3 for the specific fix (source pool noodles off-site).

## SECTION 3 — SUBSTITUTION NOTES

**Pool noodles — DO NOT BUY AT HOME DEPOT.** Home Depot only stocks pool noodles as 12-packs (~$48.70–$49.98 = ~$4/noodle). This design needs 6. **Buy 6 individual noodles from Dollar Tree, Walmart, or Target instead** — seasonal in-store pricing there typically runs $1–$2/noodle, i.e. ~$6–$12 total for exactly the quantity needed, versus $48.70 for a 12-pack of which half goes unused. This single substitution is the difference between the design hitting its ~$36 cost target and blowing past $90. No functional downside — any standard pool noodle works for this application.

**Aluminum corner bracket — no pre-drilled aluminum bracket exists at Home Depot in this size.** Search only returned zinc-plated steel corner braces (not acceptable per CLAUDE.md's non-corroding hardware rule) or aluminum in raw angle-stock/T-slot-extrusion form. Substitute: buy the **Everbilt 1"×3'×1/16" aluminum angle stock ($9.82)** and cut+drill the one L-bracket needed for the mast mount. This is a NO-substitute-acceptable item for anything touching the splash zone — zinc-plated steel corner braces WILL rust with repeated pool exposure; raw aluminum angle is the only compliant option found.

**Zip ties — 6" length not available at Home Depot.** Only 4", 8", 11", 14", 24" are stocked. Substitute: use the **8" UV-resistant 100-pack ($12.46)** for all noodle lashing — 8" comfortably wraps 3 bundled noodles around a core rod, so this is a strict upgrade, not a compromise. The same pack also covers the deck-to-bundle lashing (replacing pipe straps — see the note under Section 2's table), with ~68 ties left over.

**JB Weld MarineWeld — NO substitute acceptable for structural wet joints**, per standing project rule. The correct blue-packaging MarineWeld product (SKU 50172, $7.98) was found directly at Home Depot — no fallback needed. Regular JB Weld gray is NOT acceptable. (Not currently used structurally in this design — see contingency note above.)

**Marine silicone sealant** — DAP Ultra Clear Waterproof Sealant ($13.98) is an acceptable marine-rated silicone; GE Advanced Silicone 2 ($10.98) is also acceptable per spec (GE Silicone II family) and is the cheaper option if clear/UV-clarity isn't critical for gland sealing.

**M4 stainless hardware — no substitute needed.** Genuine M4-0.7 A2-70 stainless screws (Prime-Line, $4.12/10-pack) were found directly at Home Depot; no fallback to M3 or #6-32 required.

**Plywood — "in-store cut to size" service/fee could not be confirmed** from the search results pages. Call ahead or ask a Building Materials associate on arrival; if free/cheap, have them cut the panel down and take the offcut home anyway (it's reusable stock either way).

## SECTION 4 — BUILD SEQUENCE

1. **Cut all pieces first** (Home Depot, or at home with basic tools): 6× 500mm noodle segments, 2× 500mm PVC core rods, 1× 350×200mm plywood deck panel (+ save the offcut), 1× 500mm dowel mast, 1× ~3" aluminum L-bracket cut and drilled from the angle stock.
2. **Seal the wood** (same day, passive dry time): spray the plywood deck (both faces + all cut edges) and the dowel mast with the Spar Urethane. Let dry per can instructions (typically a few hours to overnight) — this is the only cure-adjacent wait in the whole build, and it's a coat, not a structural bond, so it doesn't block the next step by much.
3. **Assemble the pontoons**: thread each core rod through 3 noodles, bundle triangularly, lash with 8" zip ties every ~120mm along the length.
4. **Mount bundles to deck**: position the two lashed pontoon assemblies parallel at 350mm beam (rod centerline to rod centerline) under the deck; drill a pair of small holes through the deck at each of 4 lash points (2 per pontoon), thread zip ties down around each bundle and back up, cinch tight.
5. **Mount the mast**: attach the drilled aluminum L-bracket to the deck with wood screws, bolt/screw the dowel mast to the bracket, confirm vertical with a level.
6. **Mount the enclosure**: bolt the IP67 enclosure to the deck using the M4 stainless screws + washers (through the enclosure's own mounting flange/standoffs).
7. **Cable gland sealing** (separate task, done when wiring the enclosure): apply the DAP marine silicone around each of the 7 gland penetrations per the existing `hull_construction_guide.md` Phase 9 procedure — this step doesn't change with the hull material.
8. **Float test**: place the bare hull in a tub/pool before final electronics load, confirm waterline and trim, mark the actual waterline on the noodles.

**No structural cure-time gates.** The only passive wait is the wood sealant drying (hours, not days), which can happen the same afternoon as cutting. This entire build is realistically a single afternoon, in contrast to the PVC baseline's two separate 24-hour epoxy cure windows.

## SECTION 5 — REUSE AFTER DEMO

| Material | After Aug 10 |
|---|---|
| Pool noodles (6 used of 6 bought off-site) | Pool toys / packing foam / future cheap flotation for any project. Essentially disposable-cost, fully reusable in function. |
| ½" PVC core rods (2 used of a 10ft stick) | ~9ft of ½" Sch 40 PVC left over — generic plumbing/project stock, keep in Dad's shop supply. |
| ¾" plywood deck (1 small panel cut from a 2×4ft project panel) | Large offcut remains — genuinely useful shelf/jig/enclosure-mount material. This is the single most reusable item in the whole list. |
| ¾" dowel mast | If retired, it's just a hardwood dowel — closet rod, tool handle stock, craft material. |
| Aluminum angle stock (1 bracket cut from a 3ft length) | ~2.75ft of aluminum angle left over — genuinely useful for future brackets/frames; aluminum doesn't corrode in storage. |
| M4 screws/washers, zip ties | All sold in packs far larger than needed — leftover hardware goes straight into Dad's parts bin for future use. |
| Spar urethane spray can | Partially used; keep for any future outdoor wood project. |

**Bottom line on reuse:** every material in this design outlives the demo in some useful form. Nothing here becomes hull-shaped landfill the way a cemented/capped 4" PVC pontoon would.

**🔴 OVER THE $60 HULL BUDGET FLAG.** Even with the cheaper foam-and-deck design, off-site noodle sourcing, and dropping the unnecessary pipe straps, the realistic materials cost is **~$86–92 for hull structure alone — roughly 1.4–1.5× the $60 guideline**, driven almost entirely by Home Depot's fixed pack sizes (a $24 minimum plywood panel, $10 hardware packs, a $15 spray can) rather than the design being materially wasteful. Two ways to bring it down if $60 is a hard ceiling:
1. **Skip Home Depot's plywood/dowel/hardware entirely if Dad's shop has scrap plywood, a dowel, or a hardware bin already** — the design only needs a ~350×200mm scrap of ¾" ply, a ~500mm dowel, a few screws, none of which need to be store-fresh. This is very plausibly already-owned material given "Dad: hull fabrication" is a listed collaborator role.
2. **Split hardware costs against the general project hardware budget, not the hull line** — items like M4 stainless screws, washers, and zip ties are general-purpose fasteners the project needs regardless of hull material, not hull-specific spend.

If neither applies and everything is bought fresh at Home Depot, budget **~$100–110 out the door** for this design — still meaningfully cheaper than a from-scratch 4" PVC build with full-price fittings/end caps/cement (~$200+, per the previous list), but above the originally stated $60 hull target.
